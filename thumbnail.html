What to fix

    📚

    Thumbnail wisdom what VidIQ + top creators have proven works

    These are the rules the scoring rubric is built on. Each one comes from observed CTR data on hundreds of thousands of YouTube + Shorts thumbnails. Click any card to expand the details.

    😱Faces with emotion+30% CTR

    Thumbnails with a clear face — especially showing shock, joy, disgust, awe, or fear — outperform face-less thumbnails by ~30%. Brains are wired to lock onto faces in the periphery; emotion adds urgency.

    • Do: Crop the face large (1/4 to 1/3 of the thumbnail). Eyes looking AT the camera or AT the subject of interest.
    • Don't: Tiny face in corner, neutral expression, sunglasses, fully obscured face.
    • VTubers: Your model counts — use the most expressive frame from your stream. Auto face detection misses 2D / illustrated models, so the character checklist in Step 2 lets you mark what's actually in your frame.
    🔤Text: 2–4 words MAXreadable at 120px

    YouTube mobile thumbnails render at ~120×68px. If your text isn't readable at that size, it's noise. The sweet spot is 2–4 huge words.

    • Do: "I QUIT", "FACE REVEAL", "$10K BUILD", "THIS IS WHY".
    • Don't: Full sentences, watermark text, channel name across the thumbnail.
    • Font: Bold sans-serif (Impact, Anton, Bebas Neue). Outline + drop shadow so it survives any background.
    🌈High contrast + saturationscroll-stopping

    The mobile feed is a stack of muddy, low-contrast images. A saturated, high-contrast thumbnail interrupts the scroll. Black-to-white luminance variance > 0.18 is the magic number.

    • Do: Bright accent colors (hot pink, neon green, electric blue) against dark/muted backgrounds.
    • Don't: All pastels. All dark. All one hue. Grey-on-grey.
    • Tip: Use complementary colors — orange + blue, purple + yellow, red + cyan. They vibrate against each other.
    🎯One clear focal pointrule of thirds

    The eye should know IMMEDIATELY where to look. One subject, dominantly placed. Anything else is noise.

    • Do: Place the subject at a rule-of-thirds intersection, not dead center (unless the composition demands it).
    • Don't: 5 different elements all fighting for attention. Crowded backgrounds. Multiple focal points.
    • Test: Squint at the thumbnail from 6 feet away. Can you tell what it's about in 0.5 seconds? If not, simplify.
    📱Mobile-first: 120×68px test75% of views

    ~75% of YouTube views come from mobile. Most viewers see your thumbnail at 120×68px in the feed. If it doesn't pop at that size, it doesn't matter how good the desktop version looks.

    • Do: Test by scrolling past it on your own phone. Does it stop you?
    • Don't: Pack details only visible at 1280px. The duration badge (bottom right) and progress bar cover the bottom 10% — keep critical text out of there.
    • Tip: The mobile preview above this page shows you exactly how it'll render.
    🎨Pattern interruptscuriosity gap

    Best-performing thumbnails break a visual or contextual expectation. Something is "off" in a way that demands explanation.

    • Visual: Subject in an unexpected location, impossible scale, dramatic lighting, oversized prop.
    • Contextual: A face that doesn't match what the title implies. A title that contradicts the image.
    • Avoid clichés that have stopped working: Generic red arrows. Open-mouth shock that looks fake. The "circled face" trope without purpose.
    🟦Edge safety: stay 10% insidevertical-safe

    YouTube and other platforms crop thumbnails differently across surfaces (especially Shorts vs long-form vs Twitch). Keep your face + text in the inner 80% of the canvas — the outer 10% on every side may get cropped.

    • Bottom: duration badge eats the lower-right corner.
    • Top: some platforms overlay a "new" badge or live indicator.
    • Tip: If your subject's eyes are off-screen on the YT mockup above, you've got an edge-safety problem.
    ⚖️Match the title's promiseretention > clicks

    A clickbait thumbnail with a mismatched video = high CTR, terrible retention = the algorithm punishes you. The thumbnail and the first 8 seconds of the video must deliver on the same promise.

    • Do: Use thumbnails from actual scenes IN the video. The viewer should think "yes, that's the moment I saw."
    • Don't: Bait with something that never appears. Use scenes from a totally different video.
    • Test: If a stranger saw your thumbnail and title, would they accurately predict what the video is about?
